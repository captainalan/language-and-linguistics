Which Greek?

The goal of this method is to assist you in learning that Greek which will ultimately useful for the reading and interpretation of the Septuigant. This means not only that text but also writings by Greek language authors past and present. So, there will be no direction mention of learning things that are only useful for reading ancient Greek (pagan) writings but helpful notes for learning (modern) Greek as it is used today.

The emphasis here is on the understanding of written and spoken/sung Greek. How nice would it be to hear and understand beautiful Church music, study meticulously prepared books, and pray in the language of the source text of the Holy Scriptures!

The Method

First, learn words. In reading books, you will come across Greek terms. You can learn some basic morphology, but do not first focus on "grammar". Instead, focus on learning proper meanings and their associated terms in whatever other languages you may need (e.g. English, Russian) as well as in Greek. As far as details go, know how to spell words to the level of detail you find appropriate—e.g. knowing a sequence of letters may be adequate for the level of proficiency you're concerned with as you are probably not an aspiring scholar of Ancient Greek if you're reading this page.

Proper pronunciation can be learned by observing correspondences between writing and speech. Fortunately, there are many freely available resources to do this. Searching Youtube for things read (or sung, spoken) in Modern Greek will yield many results.

Grammar can gradually be acquired, first though going through reference books to know what is "out there" (e.g. what are the different forms I will need to identify? Learning which declensions, verb conjugations, and so on I need to know about). As for the actual forms, I'll first understand meanings of sentences and then learn how to grammatically unpack words and sentences.

Computing

Learning to type Greek isn't so bad. One cool aspect of the Greek keyboard layout is that typing the requisite accent marks is very easy. Whereas in Western European langauges you might have to do some finger gymnastics to type the correct accent marks, in Greek you use the semicolon key which sits nicely on the homerow.

I only care to type monotonic Greek, associated with the modern language/spellings.

There are some really cool looking Greek fonts; see Google fonts
